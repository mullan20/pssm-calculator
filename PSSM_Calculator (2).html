Leadership Commitment to Eliminating Preventable Harm 5 statements · 1 point requires ALL affirmative

D1-A
Our hospital senior governing board prioritizes safety as a core value, holds hospital leadership accountable for patient safety, and includes patient safety metrics to inform annual leadership performance reviews and compensation.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Measure Specifications (Sept 2025)
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide (June 2025) – Domain 1 examples
  • IHI Framework for Effective Board Governance of Health System Quality (2018)
  • ▶ Video (90 sec): Board Governance & Patient Safety – IHI
  • ACHE / IHI Leading a Culture of Safety: A Blueprint for Success (2017)
  • AHRQ National Action Alliance for Patient and Workforce Safety
  • IHI Safer Together: National Action Plan to Advance Patient Safety
  • AHRQ Hospital Patient Safety Resources
Tip: Document in board minutes how patient safety metrics are used in performance reviews. The Attestation Guide provides acceptable examples of metrics used for leadership accountability.
D1-B
Our hospital leaders, including C-suite executives, place patient safety as a core institutional value. One or more C-suite leaders oversee a system-wide assessment on safety (examples provided in the Attestation Guide), and the execution of patient safety initiatives and operations, with specific improvement plans and metrics. These plans and metrics are widely shared across the hospital and governing board.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide (IQR & PCHQR) – C-suite safety assessment examples (June 2025)
  • IHI Framework for Total Systems Safety (2020)
  • IHI Assessment Tool for National Action Plan to Advance Patient Safety (Self-Assessment)
  • AHRQ: Hospital-Wide Patient Safety Assessment Tools
  • AHRQ PSNet: Safety Culture Primer
Tip: Identify your C-suite "patient safety champion" and document their oversight role in your organizational chart and meeting records. Widely sharing plans means dashboards, newsletters, or town halls — document these methods.
D1-C
Our hospital governing board, in collaboration with leadership, ensures adequate resources to support patient safety (such as equipment, training, systems, personnel, and technology).

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Resource allocation examples
  • ACHE / IHI Blueprint for Safety: Leading a Culture of Safety (2017)
  • IHI Safer Together: Foundational Elements for Safety
  • HQIN Quick Start Guide: PSSM Domain 1
Tip: Document board-approved budget line items dedicated to patient safety (FTE positions, training programs, EHR upgrades, safety technology). Board meeting minutes are key evidence here.
D1-D
Reporting on patient and workforce safety events and initiatives (such as safety outcomes, improvement work, risk assessments, event cause analysis, infection outbreak, culture of safety, or other patient safety topics) accounts for at least 20% of the regular board agenda and discussion time for senior governing board meetings.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Board agenda time examples
  • Gordon, S. How to Make the Most of Your Board's Time. AHA (2019)
  • AHA Center for Health Innovation: Patient Safety Governance
Tip: Review your last 4 board meeting agendas and time-stamp safety agenda items. The 20% threshold applies to the regular governing board — not a safety subcommittee. Timed agendas are helpful documentation.
D1-E
C-suite executives and individuals on the governing board are notified within 3 business days of any confirmed serious safety events resulting in significant morbidity, mortality, or other harm.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Serious safety event definition & notification examples
  • Joint Commission: 2025 Updates to Sentinel Events & Serious Reportable Events (JC & NQF)
  • Joint Commission: 2026 Sentinel Event Policy and Procedures
  • HPI White Paper: Safety Event Classification (SEC) & Serious Safety Event Rate (SSER) (2011)
  • ASHRM White Paper Series: Serious Safety Events – Getting to Zero (2014)
  • NAIC Guideline for Implementation of Medical Professional Liability Closed Claim Reporting (2010)
Tip: Create a written policy defining "confirmed serious safety event" and the escalation pathway (who notifies whom, via what channel, within what timeframe). The 3-business-day clock starts at confirmation, not discovery. Use the Joint Commission Sentinel Event definitions to determine what qualifies.

Strategic Planning & Organizational Policy 5 statements · 1 point requires ALL affirmative
0 / 5 Yes
D2-A
Our hospital has a strategic plan that publicly shares its commitment to patient safety as a core value and outlines specific safety goals and associated metrics, including the goal of "zero preventable harm."

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Strategic plan examples & zero harm language
  • ACHE Blueprint for Safety: Leading a Culture of Safety (IHI/ACHE)
  • AHRQ PSNet: Comprehensive Unit-Based Safety Program (CUSP) Method
  • Johns Hopkins Armstrong Institute: CUSP Implementation Tools & Guidance
  • IHI Safer Together: National Action Plan (Strategic Planning Framework)
  • AHRQ: Developing Patient Safety Goals & Metrics
Tip: "Publicly shares" means accessible to staff, patients, and the public — e.g., on your website or in an annual report. The strategic plan must explicitly use "zero preventable harm" language and list associated metrics (e.g., CLABSI rates, fall rates). ASHRM's Getting to Zero™ initiative is a recognized framework for the zero harm goal.
D2-B
Our hospital safety goals include the use of metrics to identify and address gaps in safety outcomes based on the patient characteristics determined by the hospital to be most important to health care outcomes for the specific populations served.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Equity stratification examples (Domain 2B)
  • NHS Patient Safety Healthcare Inequalities Reduction Framework: 5 Principles (2025)
  • AHRQ: Health Equity Resources & Disparities Data
  • CMS Health Equity: Stratified Data Resources
Tip: This requires stratifying your safety metrics by patient characteristics such as race, ethnicity, language, age, payer, or socioeconomic factors — whichever are most relevant to your patient population. Document the stratification analysis and any resulting interventions. Note: the CMS spec uses "disparities" to describe the gaps this statement is designed to surface.
D2-C
Our hospital has implemented written policies and protocols to cultivate a "just culture" that balances no-blame and appropriate accountability and reflects the distinction between human error, at-risk behavior, and reckless behavior.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Just culture policy examples
  • ▶ Video (4 min): Sidney Dekker on Just Culture
  • AHRQ CANDOR Toolkit (supports just culture implementation)
  • AHRQ PSNet: Just Culture Primer
  • IHI: Just Culture – Framework & Tools
Tip: Your written policy must explicitly reference all three behavioral categories: human error (console/support), at-risk behavior (coach/train), and reckless behavior (discipline). The David Marx "Just Culture" algorithm is a widely accepted framework. Document training on this policy for all staff.
D2-D
Our hospital requires implementation of a patient safety curriculum and competencies for all clinical and non-clinical hospital staff, including C-suite executives and individuals on the governing board, regular assessments of these competencies for all roles, and action plans for advancing safety skills and behaviors.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Curriculum & competency examples
  • AHRQ National Action Alliance: Healthcare Safety Competencies by Profession (Resource List)
  • AHRQ TeamSTEPPS: Team Training for Patient Safety
  • IHI Open School: Patient Safety Curriculum & Modules
  • NAHQ: Healthcare Quality Competency Framework (2019)
Tip: The curriculum must cover ALL staff — this includes board members and C-suite, not just clinical staff. Document completion rates, competency assessment tools, and action plans for gaps. TeamSTEPPS is a commonly cited curriculum that meets this requirement.
D2-E
Our hospital has an action plan for workforce safety with improvement activities, metrics and trends that address issues such as slips/trips/falls prevention, safe patient handling, exposures, sharps injuries, violence prevention, fire/electrical safety, and psychological safety.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

Tip: The list in the attestation statement is not exhaustive — your plan must address the specific hazards relevant to your workforce. The plan must include measurable metrics and trends over time, not just policies. Psychological safety is explicitly included, connecting to your Just Culture work in D2-C.

Culture of Safety & Learning Health System 5 statements · 1 point requires ALL affirmative
0 / 5 Yes
D3-A
Our hospital conducts a hospital-wide culture of safety survey using a validated instrument annually, or every 2 years with pulse surveys on target units during non-survey years. Results are shared with the governing board and hospital staff and used to inform unit-based interventions to reduce harm.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • AHRQ Surveys on Patient Safety Culture (SOPS) – Hospital Survey (HSOPSC)
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Validated survey examples
  • Leapfrog Hospital Safety Grades – Safety Culture Component
  • Press Ganey Safety Culture Survey (validated instrument)
Tip: "Validated instrument" means a psychometrically validated tool — AHRQ's HSOPSC is the most commonly used and free. The biennial option requires pulse surveys in off-years on target units. Document that results were formally shared with the board and that unit-level action plans were created.
D3-B
Our hospital has a dedicated team that conducts event analysis of serious safety events using an evidence-based approach, such as the National Patient Safety Foundation's Root Cause Analysis and Action (RCA²).

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • IHI: RCA² – Improving Root Cause Analyses and Actions to Prevent Harm
  • AHRQ PSNet: Root Cause Analysis Primer
  • AHRQ: Common Cause Analysis – Advances in Patient Safety (Vol. 1)
  • IHI White Paper: Respectful Management of Serious Clinical Adverse Events (2011)
  • Joint Commission: Sentinel Event Analysis Procedures
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Event analysis team examples
Tip: "Dedicated team" means an identified, consistent group trained in RCA² or equivalent. Document team members, training records, and completed analyses. RCA² is preferred over traditional RCA as it emphasizes action planning with accountability and deadlines. Common Cause Analysis is useful for identifying systemic patterns across multiple events.
D3-C
Our hospital has a patient safety metrics dashboard and uses external benchmarks (such as CMS Star Ratings or other national databases) to monitor performance and inform improvement activities on safety events (such as: medication errors, surgical/procedural harm, falls, pressure injuries, diagnostic errors, and healthcare-associated infections).

Tip: Your dashboard must include external benchmarks — not just internal trend data. CMS Star Ratings, NHSN HAI comparisons, AHRQ PSIs, and CAHPS are all acceptable. Document how dashboard insights led to specific improvement initiatives for each safety event type.
D3-D
Our hospital implements a minimum of 4 of the following high reliability practices:
  • Tiered and escalating safety huddles at least 5 days a week (including 1 weekend day) that include key clinical and non-clinical units and leaders, with a method for follow-up on issues identified.
  • Hospital leaders participate in monthly rounding for safety on all units, with C-suite executives rounding at least quarterly, with a method for follow-up on issues identified.
  • A data infrastructure to measure safety based on patient safety evidence and EMR data that enables identification and tracking of serious and precursor safety events — shared with C-suite at least monthly and the governing board at every regularly scheduled meeting.
  • Technologies including a computerized physician order entry (CPOE) system and a barcode medication administration (BCMA) system that promote safety and standardization using evidence-based practices.
  • The use of a defined improvement method (or hybrid), such as Lean, Six Sigma, Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA), and/or high reliability frameworks.
  • Team communication and collaboration training of all staff.
  • The use of human factors engineering principles in selection and design of devices, equipment, and processes.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – High reliability practice examples
  • Safety Huddles:
  • AHRQ PSNet: Improving Patient Safety Through Daily Huddles
  • IHI Safety Huddles Tool & Implementation Guide
  • ▶ Video (1 min): IHI Safety Huddles in Action
  • Leadership Rounding:
  • IHI Patient Safety Leadership WalkRounds Tool
  • Improvement Methods (Lean / Six Sigma / PDSA / HRO):
  • IHI Framework for Safe, Reliable and Effective Care (2017)
  • American Society for Quality: PDSA/PDCA Cycle
  • Joint Commission: High Reliability – A Practical Framework for Improvement
  • IHI Patient Safety Essentials Toolkit (9 tools incl. RCA², SBAR, FMEA, Huddles)
  • Team Communication Training:
  • AHRQ TeamSTEPPS (Team Strategies & Tools to Enhance Performance & Patient Safety)
  • Blueprint for Clinical Event Debriefing Program Implementation
  • Human Factors Engineering:
  • AHRQ Primer: Human Factors Engineering in Healthcare (2024)
  • General HRO:
  • AHRQ CUSP: Comprehensive Unit-Based Safety Program
Tip: You need at least 4 of the 7 listed practices. Assess which you currently have fully implemented vs. partially in place. For huddles: document the schedule, attendance rosters, and the follow-up tracking method. For leadership rounding: show sign-in logs and a tracking system. The IHI Patient Safety Essentials Toolkit covers multiple sub-practices in one place.
D3-E
Our hospital participates in large-scale learning network(s) for patient safety improvement (such as national or state safety improvement collaboratives), shares data on safety events and outcomes with these network(s), and has implemented at least one best practice from the network or collaborative.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • Solutions for Patient Safety (SPS) National Children's Hospital Network
  • IHI Learning Networks & Collaboratives
  • HQIN: Hospital Quality Improvement Network (CMS BFCC-QIO)
  • CDC NHSN: National HAI Prevention Collaborative
  • AHRQ: State & National Patient Safety Collaboratives
Tip: Your hospital's NHSN participation may count as a learning network if data is actively shared. State Hospital Associations often run quality collaboratives that qualify. Document: network name, data-sharing agreements, and the specific best practice you adopted with implementation evidence.

D4-A
Our hospital has a confidential safety reporting system that allows staff to report patient safety events, near misses, precursor events, unsafe conditions, and other concerns, and prompts a feedback loop to those who report.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • AHRQ PSNet: Reporting Patient Safety Events Primer
  • NHS Patient Safety Incident Response Framework (Feedback Loop Guidance, 2025)
  • AHRQ PSO Program – Legal protection for confidential reporting
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Safety reporting system examples
Tip: The feedback loop is critical — staff must receive acknowledgment and information about what happened after they report. Document your feedback process (e.g., automated acknowledgment emails, unit-based safety sharing sessions). Systems like RL Solutions, Quantros, or Datix are common examples. Pairing your reporting system with PSO membership (D4-B) provides added legal protection.
D4-B
Our hospital voluntarily works with a Patient Safety Organization (PSO) listed by the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) to carry out patient safety activities as described in 42 CFR 3.20, such as the collection and analysis of patient safety work product, dissemination of information such as best practices, encouraging a culture of safety, or activities related to the operation of a patient safety evaluation system.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • AHRQ: Federally Listed Patient Safety Organizations Directory (filtered by PSO services)
  • AHRQ PSO Program: Overview, Benefits & How to Engage
  • 42 CFR Part 3 – Patient Safety and Quality Improvement Act Regulations
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – PSO participation examples
Tip: If your hospital doesn't currently have a PSO agreement, this is a foundational step. Use the AHRQ directory (filtered link above) to identify a listed PSO that provides the specific services described in 42 CFR 3.20. PSO participation provides legal protection for patient safety work product. Document your signed agreement and active participation activities.
D4-C
Patient safety metrics are tracked and reported to all clinical and non-clinical staff and made public in hospital units (for example, displayed on units so that staff, patients, families, and visitors can see).

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Transparency and display examples
  • Staff Reporting / Visual Management:
  • AHRQ Visual Management Board Component Kit
  • Public Reporting:
  • IHI / NPSF: Shining a Light – Safer Health Care Through Transparency (2015)
  • CMS Care Compare: Public Reporting Standards
Tip: Physical unit-level safety boards or digital displays qualify. The key is that metrics are accessible to patients and visitors — not just staff. Document locations, what metrics are shown, and update frequency. Non-clinical areas (housekeeping, food service) should also receive appropriate safety metric updates.
D4-D
Our hospital has a defined, evidence-based communication and resolution program reliably implemented after harm events, such as AHRQ's CANDOR toolkit, that contains all of the following elements:
  • Harm event identification
  • Open and ongoing communication with patients and families about the harm event
  • Event investigation, prevention, and learning
  • Care-for-the-caregiver
  • Financial and non-financial reconciliation
  • Patient-family engagement and ongoing support

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • AHRQ CANDOR Toolkit – Complete Overview & All Elements
  • AHRQ CANDOR: Implementation Guide (all 6 required elements)
  • ▶ Video (11 min): AHRQ CANDOR Introduction
  • AHRQ CANDOR Video Introduction (hosted on AHRQ)
  • Oregon Patient Safety Commission: PACT Program (Communication & Resolution)
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – CRP examples & definitions
Tip: All 6 elements must be present. CANDOR is the gold standard and free to adopt. "Care-for-the-caregiver" (second victim support) is often the missing element — ensure your program explicitly includes peer support, EAP referrals, or caregiver debriefing after harm events. "Reliably implemented" means every time, not ad hoc.
D4-E
Our hospital uses standard measures to track the performance of our communication and resolution program and reports these measures to the governing board at least quarterly.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • AHRQ CANDOR: Measurement Framework for CRP Performance
  • AHRQ PSNet: Communication and Resolution Programs Primer
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – CRP performance measure examples
Tip: Standard measures may include: time to initial disclosure, rate of CRP activation, patient/family satisfaction with disclosure, caregiver support utilization rate, and cases reaching financial resolution. These must be formally presented to the governing board at least 4 times per year — document in board minutes.

Patient & Family Engagement 5 statements · 1 point requires ALL affirmative
0 / 5 Yes
D5-A
Our hospital has a Patient and Family Advisory Council that ensures patient, family, caregiver, and community input to safety-related activities, including representation at board meetings, consultation on safety goal-setting and metrics, and participation in safety improvement initiatives.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • AHRQ Guide for Developing a Community-Based Patient Safety Advisory Council
  • AHA Patient and Family Advisory Council (PFAC) Blueprint (2022)
  • IPFCC: How to Start a Patient & Family Advisory Council
  • AHRQ: Guide to Patient and Family Engagement in Hospital Safety
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – PFAC examples & board representation
Tip: Board representation means PFAC members are actually present at (or formally report to) governing board meetings — not just hospital committee meetings. Document PFAC meeting records, the safety topics addressed, and how their input changed decisions or goals.
D5-B
Our hospital's Patient and Family Advisory Council includes patients and caregivers of patients who are representative of the patient population.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • IPFCC: Diverse Voices Matter – Advancing Diversity in PFACs
  • IPFCC: Advancing Diversity, Inclusion & Equity in PFACs
  • AHRQ: Recruiting Diverse Patient & Family Advisors
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – PFAC representativeness examples
Tip: Compare your PFAC's demographic composition to your hospital's patient population data (race, ethnicity, language, age, payer mix, clinical service lines). Document your intentional recruitment efforts to fill representation gaps. "Representative" is assessed by your hospital's specific patient population, not national averages.
D5-C
Patients have comprehensive access to and are encouraged to view their own medical records and clinician notes via patient portals and other options, and the hospital provides support to help patients interpret information that is culturally and linguistically appropriate, as well as submit comments for potential correction to their record.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • AMA Patient Access Playbook: Legal Requirements for Patient Record Access
  • OpenNotes: Improving Patient Safety Through Open Records
  • ONC Health IT: Patient Access to Health Information (21st Century Cures Act)
  • CMS Interoperability & Patient Access Final Rule
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Patient portal access examples
Tip: Under the 21st Century Cures Act, information blocking is already prohibited. For PSSM, you must also show you actively encourage patients to use their portal. Document portal enrollment rates, language support options (translated materials, interpreter services), and your process for patients to submit record corrections.
D5-D
Our hospital incorporates patient and caregiver input about patient safety events or issues (such as patient submission of safety events, safety signals from patient complaints or other patient safety experience data).

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • AHRQ Guide to Patient and Family Engagement in Hospital Quality and Safety
  • AHRQ: Patient & Family Engagement in Safety Event Reporting
  • AHRQ PSNet: Patient Engagement in Patient Safety Primer
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Patient input integration examples (incl. discrimination reports)
Tip: This requires a formal process — not just responding to complaints. Options include: a patient-facing safety event report button in your portal, grievance data systematically reviewed for safety signals, HCAHPS safety domain analysis informing improvement work, or PFAC review of safety complaint patterns. Note: the CMS spec also includes patient reports of discrimination as a category of patient safety input. Document the process and an example of patient input that led to a change.
D5-E
Our hospital supports the presence of family and other designated persons (as defined by the patient) as essential members of a safe care team and encourages engagement in activities such as bedside rounding and shift reporting, discharge planning, and visitation 24 hours a day, as feasible.

📋 Helpful resources for this statement

  • Wash U Internal Medicine: Embracing Caregivers in Patient-Centered Rounds
  • AHRQ: Nurse Bedside Shift Report Implementation Handbook
  • AHRQ IDEAL Discharge Planning Implementation Handbook
  • HRC: Protecting Visitation & Decision-Making Rights
  • IPFCC: Visitation Policy & Family Presence Resources
  • CMS: Patient Visitation Rights (CoP §482.13(h))
  • CMS PSSM Attestation Guide – Family presence & engagement examples
Tip: "As feasible" allows clinical judgment exceptions (e.g., infection control, patient preference, procedural areas). The key is having a written policy that defaults to open visitation and family inclusion, with exceptions documented. Shift reporting and bedside rounding with family require staff training — document this training and your policy. CMS CoP already requires hospitals to allow a support person of the patient's choice.
